The relationship between income level and the frequency of parents checking their Child’s online system




Since the calculated correlation between a parent’s income and how often they check their child’s online grading system revealed a number 0.42, it indicates a moderate correlation between the two variables (See Figure 1 above). From the pivot table, we found that parents’ income level directly impacts their frequency of checking children’s online grading system. Among the participants that earn less than $20,000 a year, all of them indicated that there is an online grading system available in their child’s school. Despite the fact that they are aware of this accessible tool, it is plausible to assume that low-income tend to have a lower level of education in which hinders their usage of tools such as online grade book systems. As a result, their frequency of using the tool is significantly lower than higher income families. In fact, more than 70% of the respondents answered that they never or rarely check their children’s online grade book system such as Pupilpath (See Figure 1). In summary, parents that earn a lower salary disregard the useful tool provided such as online grade book, and therefore, are less likely to be aware of their children’s academic performance.

Which of the following best describes why you do not attend your child's school event (Circle one)?




With only 19.8% of parents responding that they usually attend their child’s school event, 80.2% of parents reported otherwise (see Figure 3). For example, 41.1% of the parents did not attend their child's school events due to a schedule conflict, and 12.9% responded that they are afraid that no interpreters would be available. These responses made up more than half of the reason why parents are not involving in school events. Furthermore, aside from the “other” option with only 3.5% response, the least popular reason why parents do not attend school event is due to the lack of information on the events (5.4%). In other words, most parents are informed about school events. Overall, the data suggest that while most parents were given information about school events, they are unable to attend school activity due to possible conflicts with their schedules and the concern over the availability of translators. As a result, if parents are able to be excused from work, and the school is able to inform parents that translators are available, parental participation in school events will likely increase.
